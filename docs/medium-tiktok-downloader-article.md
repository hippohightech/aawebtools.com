TikTok has over 1.5 billion monthly active users in 2026, and the platform produces some of the most creative short-form content on the internet. But saving that content for offline viewing, archiving, or repurposing remains unnecessarily difficult.

The built-in download button -- when it even appears -- stamps a watermark across the video. Slideshow-format posts cannot be saved as individual photos at all. Private or story content disappears with no native save option. And if a creator disables downloads on their video, you are simply out of luck within the app.

Whether you are a content creator building a swipe file, a social media manager archiving campaign content, a researcher collecting data, or just someone who wants to save a recipe video to watch in the kitchen without burning through mobile data, you have probably searched for a TikTok downloader at some point.

I tested seven different methods using a consistent set of 20 TikTok videos -- standard videos, slideshows, HD content, and stories -- and ranked them based on reliability, quality, ease of use, and privacy. Here is what I found.

## Methodology

To keep this comparison fair, I tested each tool against the same 20 TikTok posts:

- **8 standard videos** (ranging from 15 seconds to 3 minutes)
- **4 slideshow posts** (photo carousels with music)
- **4 HD videos** (1080p source quality)
- **2 story posts** (24-hour ephemeral content)
- **2 videos with downloads disabled** by the creator

For each tool, I evaluated:

- **Success rate** -- how many of the 20 test items downloaded correctly
- **Output quality** -- whether the downloaded file matched the source resolution
- **Watermark removal** -- whether the TikTok watermark was present in the output
- **Slideshow handling** -- whether photo slideshows could be saved as individual images
- **Speed** -- time from pasting the URL to having the file
- **Privacy** -- whether a signup was required, what data the tool collects, and whether ads compromised the experience
- **Platform** -- web, mobile app, or desktop

**Limitations:** Heavy advertising, slideshow downloads are video-only (no photo extraction), occasional server errors requiring retry.

### #7. Screen Recording

**Success rate:** 20/20 (technically)

The nuclear option. Screen recording works 100% of the time because it captures whatever is on your screen regardless of download restrictions, DRM, or platform limitations.

On iOS, use the built-in screen recorder (swipe down from control center). On Android, most devices have a native screen recorder in quick settings. On desktop, OBS Studio is free and reliable.

The reason this ranks last is quality. Screen recording captures at your display resolution, not the source resolution. A 1080p TikTok video recorded on a phone screen will look noticeably worse than a direct download. You also capture the TikTok UI elements -- the watermark, the like/comment buttons, the caption overlay -- unless you go full screen first. Audio quality depends on your device's internal audio routing, which is not always straightforward.

For slideshows, you would need to manually swipe through each photo and screenshot individually, which is tedious.

Screen recording is the method of last resort. It always works, but the output quality makes it the worst option when any other method is available.

**Limitations:** Worst output quality, captures UI elements and watermark, tedious for slideshows, audio routing can be complicated.

## Legal Considerations

A quick but important note on legality. Downloading TikTok videos exists in a legal gray area that depends on your jurisdiction and intent:

- **Personal use** -- saving videos for offline viewing is generally considered acceptable, similar to recording a TV show on a DVR.
- **Content archiving** -- researchers and journalists often download social media content for documentation purposes. This is typically protected under fair use or equivalent doctrines.
- **Reposting without credit** -- downloading someone's video and reuploading it as your own is a copyright violation. Do not do this.
- **Commercial use** -- using downloaded content in ads, products, or monetized content without the creator's permission is copyright infringement.
- **Creator consent** -- the safest approach is always to contact the creator and ask permission before using their content beyond personal viewing.

TikTok's Terms of Service technically prohibit downloading content through third-party tools. Whether this is enforceable varies by jurisdiction, but it is worth being aware of.

Respect creators. If you use someone's content, credit them. If they have disabled downloads, consider whether they might have a reason for that before circumventing the restriction.
