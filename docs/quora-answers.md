## Can teachers actually detect ChatGPT in student essays?

The short answer is: sometimes, but far less reliably than most students fear and most teachers claim.

AI detection tools like GPTZero, Turnitin's AI detector, and Originality.ai use statistical models that analyze writing patterns -- things like sentence structure predictability, word choice distribution, and what is called "perplexity" (how surprising or creative the language is). AI-generated text tends to be more uniform and predictable than human writing.

**Here is the accuracy problem.** Most detectors hover around 70-85% accuracy under ideal conditions. That sounds decent until you consider what the errors look like:

- **False positives** are the real issue. Multiple studies in 2025-2026 have shown that ESL (English as a Second Language) students get flagged at significantly higher rates. Their writing tends to use simpler, more formulaic structures -- exactly the patterns detectors associate with AI. This is a serious equity problem.
- **False negatives** happen when students lightly edit AI output, use AI humanizer tools, or prompt ChatGPT to write in a more casual or intentionally imperfect style. A few strategic typos and colloquialisms can drop detection scores dramatically.
- **Mixed content** (partly human, partly AI) confuses most detectors. If a student writes their own outline and then has ChatGPT flesh out two paragraphs, detection becomes a coin flip.

What actually works better for teachers is looking for **contextual signals**: a sudden jump in writing quality compared to in-class work, vocabulary that does not match the student's speaking level, or references to concepts not covered in class.

I wrote a deeper analysis of the detection accuracy landscape with specific data: [Can Teachers Detect ChatGPT in 2026?](https://aawebtools.com/blog/can-teachers-detect-chatgpt-2026/)

The bottom line for students: do not assume detectors cannot catch you, but also know that getting flagged is not proof of anything. The bottom line for teachers: use detectors as one data point among many, never as a verdict on their own. Pair detection tools with in-class writing samples and oral follow-ups for a much more reliable assessment.

## What's the best way to compress an image to under 100KB?

The approach depends on your starting point -- a 500KB JPEG is a different problem than a 15MB PNG.

**Step 1: Choose the right format.** This is where most people lose the easy wins.

- **JPEG** is best for photographs and images with gradual color transitions. At quality 70-80%, most photos look identical to the original to the human eye but are dramatically smaller.
- **WebP** gives you roughly 25-30% smaller files than JPEG at equivalent visual quality. If your use case supports it (all modern browsers do), convert to WebP first and you might hit your target without further compression.
- **PNG** is meant for graphics with sharp edges, text, and transparency. If you are trying to compress a photograph that is currently a PNG, converting it to JPEG alone might solve your problem.

**Step 2: Use a quality slider, not just "compress."** Tools that only have a "compress" button give you no control. What you want is a tool with an adjustable quality slider so you can find the sweet spot between file size and visual quality.

[AAWebTools Image Toolkit](https://aawebtools.com/image-toolkit/) handles this well -- you can adjust compression quality, resize dimensions, and convert between formats all in one step. Everything runs in your browser so images are not uploaded to any server. That matters if you are working with client photos or sensitive images.

**TinyPNG** is another reliable option, especially for PNG files. It uses smart lossy compression that reduces colors intelligently. The free version limits you to 20 images per batch and 5MB per file.

**Squoosh** (by Google) is excellent for side-by-side comparison. You can see exactly what quality loss looks like at each compression level before you download.

**Step 3: Resize before compressing.** If your image is 4000x3000 pixels and you are displaying it at 800x600 on a webpage, resize it first. Reducing dimensions before compressing quality gets you to 100KB much faster and with less visible degradation.

## How do I make a pay stub if I'm self-employed?

When you are self-employed, nobody generates pay stubs for you -- but landlords, lenders, and immigration offices still ask for them. Here is how to handle it.

**Why you might need one.** Mortgage applications and apartment rentals are the two most common situations. Lenders want to see consistent income documentation. Landlords want proof you can cover rent. Visa and immigration applications sometimes require them as well. Even some car dealerships ask for recent pay stubs when you are financing.

**What goes on a self-employed pay stub.** The basics are the same as an employer-issued stub: your name and business name, the pay period dates, gross earnings, deductions (taxes, retirement contributions, health insurance), and net pay. The difference is that you are calculating these yourself based on your actual income for that period.

**How to actually create one.** You have a few options:

**Accounting software route:** If you already use QuickBooks Self-Employed or FreshBooks, they can generate pay stubs from your income records. This works well if your bookkeeping is already set up, but it is overkill if you just need a stub for a rental application.

**Dedicated pay stub generators:** [AAWebTools Pay Stub Generator](https://aawebtools.com/paystub-generator/) lets you fill in your earnings, deductions, and tax withholdings, then generates a professional PDF. No signup needed and it does not store your financial data. You input the numbers, download the PDF, done.

**Spreadsheet method:** You can build a pay stub template in Google Sheets or Excel. It works, but the output looks less professional than a formatted PDF, and some landlords or lenders are skeptical of homemade documents.

**Important:** your pay stub numbers need to match your tax returns and bank statements. If your stub says you earned $8,000 in March but your bank deposits show $3,000, that creates problems. A pay stub is a summary of real income, not a creative writing exercise. Keep your records consistent across all documents.

## Can AI humanizers actually bypass Turnitin?

The honest answer is: partially, sometimes, and less reliably than their marketing claims suggest.

AI humanizers work by restructuring sentences, swapping vocabulary, adjusting tone, and introducing stylistic variation into AI-generated text. The goal is to make the statistical patterns look more "human" -- more unpredictable, more varied in sentence length, more idiosyncratic in word choice.

**What the data shows.** Based on tests through late 2025 and into 2026, the better humanizer tools can reduce Turnitin AI detection scores from 90%+ down to 30-50% in many cases. Some heavily processed passages can score even lower. But "reducing the score" is not the same as "bypassing." Turnitin does not just give you a single number -- it highlights specific passages and provides confidence levels. A paper where half the text is flagged at medium confidence still draws instructor attention.

**The arms race problem.** Turnitin continuously updates its detection models. A humanizer technique that works in January may get caught by March. Humanizer developers update too, but there is always a lag. You are essentially betting that the humanizer's latest update is newer than Turnitin's latest update. That is not a reliable long-term strategy.

**Quality trade-offs.** Aggressive humanization often damages the writing. You end up with awkward synonym substitutions ("utilize" instead of "use" everywhere), unnatural sentence structures, and occasionally factual errors where the humanizer rephrases something and changes the meaning. A professor who reads the paper carefully may notice the writing feels "off" even if the AI score is low.

I go deeper into specific tools and test results here: [AI Humanizers vs Turnitin in 2026](https://aawebtools.com/blog/ai-humanizers-vs-turnitin-2026/)

**My take:** if you are a student thinking about running your ChatGPT essay through a humanizer, the risk-reward is poor. The consequences of getting caught have increased at most universities, and the tools are not reliable enough to guarantee you will not get caught. If you are a content marketer using AI as a drafting tool and want to polish the output, humanizers have legitimate uses for improving readability -- just do not expect any tool to be a magic cloak.

## Are free online tools safe to use? Do they steal your data?

It depends entirely on the specific tool, and the answer ranges from "perfectly safe" to "actively harvesting your data." Here is how to evaluate what you are dealing with.

**The business model tells you a lot.** Every free tool has to make money somehow. The transparent models are advertising (you see ads on the page) and freemium (basic features free, advanced features paid). The concerning model is when there are no ads, no paid tier, and no obvious revenue source. In that case, your data may be the product -- sold to data brokers, used for AI training, or leveraged for targeted advertising profiles.

**What to look for before using any free tool:**

- **Privacy policy existence and clarity.** A tool with no privacy policy is a red flag. A tool with a clear, short privacy policy that says what it collects and what it does not is a good sign.
- **Account requirements.** Tools that demand signup for basic functionality are collecting your email at minimum. Tools that work without any account creation have less opportunity to build a profile on you.
- **Client-side vs. server-side processing.** This is the big one. Tools that process your data in your browser (client-side) never send your files to a remote server. Tools that upload your files to their servers for processing could theoretically store, analyze, or share that data.
- **HTTPS.** Any tool handling your data should use HTTPS. If the URL starts with http:// (no 's'), your data is transmitted in plain text.

**As an example of transparent practices**, [AAWebTools](https://aawebtools.com/about/) explicitly states that it does not require signup, does not store user data, and processes files client-side. That combination -- no account, no server upload, no data retention -- is about as low-risk as a free online tool can get.

**On the other end of the spectrum**, there have been documented cases of free PDF converters, file compressors, and VPN services collecting user files, logging browsing activity, and selling data to third parties.

**Practical rules I follow:**

1. Never paste passwords, API keys, or financial data into any free tool.
2. For sensitive documents (contracts, tax forms), prefer tools that process locally in the browser.
3. Check reviews and community discussion (Reddit is useful) before trusting a new tool with anything important.
4. Use a browser's developer tools (Network tab) to verify whether a tool is actually sending your data to a server.

Most free tools fall in a gray area -- they are not malicious, but they are not fully transparent either. The safest approach is to prefer tools that are upfront about their data practices and that minimize what they collect in the first place.
