Running payroll in the United Kingdom means navigating HMRC's PAYE system, calculating National Insurance contributions at multiple thresholds, managing workplace pension auto-enrolment, and potentially deducting student loan repayments. PAYE Income Tax (2025/26)

The UK operates a Pay As You Earn (PAYE) system where employers deduct income tax at source before paying employees. For the 2025/26 tax year, the income tax bands for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland are:

  • Personal Allowance: up to £12,570 at 0%
  • Basic rate: £12,571 to £50,270 at 20%
  • Higher rate: £50,271 to £125,140 at 40%
  • Additional rate: over £125,140 at 45%

The Personal Allowance is reduced by £1 for every £2 of income above £100,000, meaning it is fully withdrawn at £125,140. Scotland sets its own income tax rates with six bands ranging from a 19% starter rate to a 48% top rate on income above £125,140.

Each employee is assigned a tax code by HMRC (for example, 1257L for the standard allowance). The tax code instructs the employer on how much tax-free income to allocate before applying the relevant rate. Employers report deductions to HMRC through Real Time Information (RTI) submissions, typically filed on or before each payday.

National Insurance Contributions (NIC)

National Insurance funds the state pension, NHS, and other benefits. For 2025/26, Class 1 employee contributions are:

  • 0% on earnings up to the Primary Threshold (approximately £12,570/year)
  • 8% on earnings between the Primary Threshold and the Upper Earnings Limit (approximately £50,270/year)
  • 2% on earnings above the Upper Earnings Limit

Employer NIC rates changed significantly from April 2025. The employer rate increased to 15% (up from 13.8%) on earnings above the Secondary Threshold, which was lowered to £5,000 (down from £9,100). This represents one of the largest increases in employer costs in recent years, and accurate payslip generation must reflect these updated thresholds.

Workplace Pension Auto-Enrolment

Since 2012, UK employers must automatically enrol eligible employees into a qualifying workplace pension scheme. The minimum contribution rates are 8% of qualifying earnings, split as 5% from the employee and 3% from the employer. Qualifying earnings for 2025/26 fall between approximately £6,240 and £50,270 per year. Employees can opt out within the first month but are re-enrolled every three years.

Common pension providers include NEST (the government-backed scheme), The People's Pension, and NOW: Pensions. The employee's pension contribution is typically deducted before tax under a "net pay" arrangement or after tax under "relief at source," depending on the scheme. Payslips must show the pension deduction clearly.

Student Loan Repayment

Employees with outstanding student loans have repayments deducted through PAYE. The repayment plan determines the threshold and rate:

  • Plan 1 (pre-2012 England/Wales, all Scottish/NI loans): 9% on earnings above £24,990/year
  • Plan 2 (post-2012 England/Wales): 9% on earnings above £27,295/year
  • Plan 4 (post-2012 Scottish loans): 9% on earnings above £31,395/year
  • Plan 5 (from 2023 onwards): 9% on earnings above £25,000/year
  • Postgraduate Loan: 6% on earnings above £21,000/year

HMRC notifies employers which plan type to apply via the employee's tax code or a separate SL1/SL2 notice. Multiple plans can apply simultaneously.

What Must Appear on a UK Payslip

The Employment Rights Act 1996 requires every UK employer to provide an itemised pay statement. A compliant payslip must include:

  • Gross pay (before deductions)
  • Variable deductions with amounts: PAYE tax, employee NIC
  • Fixed deductions: pension contributions, student loan repayments
  • Net pay (take-home amount)
  • Hours worked (for hourly-paid employees, since April 2019)

While not legally required, best practice is to also show the employer's NIC contribution, pension employer contribution, tax code, NI number, and pay period dates. This transparency helps employees verify their tax position and builds trust.

Frequently Asked Questions

What deductions must appear on a UK payslip?

Under the Employment Rights Act 1996, UK employers must provide an itemised pay statement showing gross pay, variable deductions (PAYE income tax, National Insurance contributions), fixed deductions (workplace pension, student loan repayments, union dues), and net pay. Since April 2019, employers must also show total hours worked for hourly-paid employees. HMRC can issue penalties for non-compliant payslips.

How does PAYE work in the United Kingdom?

PAYE (Pay As You Earn) is the HMRC system through which employers deduct income tax and National Insurance from employee wages before paying them. Each employee has a tax code (e.g. 1257L) that tells the employer how much tax-free allowance to apply. The employer calculates the correct deduction each pay period and remits it to HMRC through Real Time Information (RTI) reporting, typically by the 22nd of the following month.

What are the 2025/26 National Insurance rates for employees?

For the 2025/26 tax year, employees pay Class 1 National Insurance at 8% on earnings between the Primary Threshold (approximately £12,570 per year) and the Upper Earnings Limit (approximately £50,270 per year), then 2% on earnings above the UEL. Employers pay 15% on earnings above the reduced Secondary Threshold of £5,000, following the increase announced in the Autumn Budget 2024.
