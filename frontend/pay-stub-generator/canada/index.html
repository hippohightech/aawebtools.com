Understanding Canadian Payroll Deductions in 2026

Canada Pension Plan (CPP / CPP2)

For 2026, employees contribute 5.95% of pensionable earnings between the basic exemption of $3,500 and the first earnings ceiling of $73,200. The maximum employee contribution for CPP1 is approximately $4,147. A second tier, CPP2, applies at 4% on earnings between $73,200 and the second ceiling of $81,200, adding up to a further $320 in contributions. Employers match both amounts dollar for dollar, making total CPP costs a significant payroll expense.

Employment Insurance (EI)

The 2026 EI premium rate for employees outside Quebec is 1.64% on insurable earnings up to $65,700, producing a maximum annual premium of roughly $1,077. Employers pay 1.4 times the employee rate. In Quebec, the employee EI rate is reduced to 1.32% because the province operates its own parental insurance plan (QPIP), which carries a separate premium of 0.494% for employees and 0.692% for employers.

Federal Income Tax

Canada uses a progressive federal tax system. For 2026, the brackets are approximately 15% on the first $57,375, 20.5% on income from $57,375 to $114,750, 26% up to $158,468, 29% up to $220,000, and 33% on income above $220,000. Every employee also receives a basic personal amount of approximately $16,129, which reduces tax on the first portion of earnings to zero.

Provincial and Territorial Tax

Each province sets its own income tax brackets. Alberta maintains a flat 10% rate on the first $148,269, while Ontario uses rates from 5.05% to 13.16% across five brackets. British Columbia ranges from 5.06% to 20.5%. Employers must calculate provincial tax based on the employee's province of employment, not residence, unless a specific agreement exists.

Quebec: A Separate Payroll System

Quebec operates outside several federal programs. Instead of CPP, Quebec employees contribute to the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) at 6.40% for 2026, with employer matching. The province administers its own parental insurance plan (QPIP/RQAP) and collects provincial income tax through Revenu Quebec rather than the CRA. Employers in Quebec must remit source deductions to both the CRA (for federal tax and EI) and Revenu Quebec (for QPP, QPIP, and provincial tax). Pay stubs for Quebec employees should clearly separate these provincial items from the federal lines.

What Must Appear on a Canadian Pay Stub

While pay stub requirements vary by province, best practice across Canada includes displaying the following on every stub:

  • Employer name and address
  • Employee name and job title
  • Pay period start and end dates, plus payment date
  • Hours worked (regular and overtime)
  • Gross earnings and itemized deductions (CPP, EI, federal tax, provincial tax)
  • Voluntary deductions such as RRSP contributions, union dues, or group benefits
  • Net pay (take-home amount)
  • Year-to-date totals for earnings and each deduction category

Provinces like British Columbia, Ontario, and Manitoba explicitly require employers to provide written pay statements. Alberta requires them upon request. Regardless of provincial rules, issuing a detailed pay stub is a best practice that protects both employer and employee.

Frequently Asked Questions

What deductions are required on a Canadian pay stub?

Canadian employers must deduct Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions, Employment Insurance (EI) premiums, and federal and provincial income tax from each employee's pay. In Quebec, employers deduct QPP, QPIP, and provincial tax instead of CPP and federal EI rates. Additional voluntary deductions may include RRSP contributions, union dues, and group benefit premiums.

What are the 2026 CPP and EI rates in Canada?

For 2026, the CPP employee contribution rate is 5.95% on pensionable earnings between $3,500 and $73,200 (CPP1), with CPP2 applying at 4% on earnings between $73,200 and $81,200. The EI employee premium rate is 1.64% on insurable earnings up to $65,700, while Quebec employees pay 1.32% for EI with an additional QPIP premium of 0.494%.

Do I need to show provincial tax separately on a Canadian pay stub?

While the CRA combines federal and provincial tax into a single withholding amount on the TD1 form, best practice for Canadian pay stubs is to show the provincial tax component separately. This gives employees clarity on their total tax burden and is required in several provinces. Quebec is unique because Revenu Quebec administers its own provincial tax, so QC employees always see separate federal and provincial lines.
